About The Project

Type 2 diabetes is a chronic disorder that results from either the body's failure to produce enough insulin, the body developing a resistance to the effects of this insulin, or both. In 2015, 415 million persons aged 20 to 79 had diabetes mellitus, according to the International Diabetes Federation (IDF). The fact that this number is anticipated to increase by another 200 million by 2040 indicates that DM is posing a burden on public health globally.

    There are several drugs (GLP-1R agonists) available in the treatment of these diseases, some of which cause severe side effects and others gradually losing their efficacy the longer a patient uses them.

Most of these drugs are administered subcutaneously meaning they are injected into a fatty tissue just below the skin. This project seeks to computationally predict active orally susceptible GLP-1R agonists by using supervised learning model, category of machine learning for this process. Dataset was acquired from an online archive of chemical molecules and their activities against biological assays (PubChem).

Dataset is then cleaned and preprocessed for training using python and models such as Random Forest (RF), XG boost, Linear regression, Artificial Neural Network and others were trained. Compounds that successfully go through end up as drugs (preferably oral) against type 2 diabetes after clinical trials and aid in the reduction of the disease.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • 1.What is a GLP-1R agonist?

    Glucagon-like peptide-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1R) are a class of medications used to treat type 2 diabetes. GLP-1 is an incretin hormone that helps the pancreas release insulin. Persons with type 2 diabetes have lower levels of incretin hormones, which lead to high blood sugar.

  • What are some examples of GLP-1R agonist?

    There are several GLP-1R agonists, some of which include: Adlyxin (lixisenatide), Bydureon (exanatide), Byetta (exanatide), Ozempic (semaglutide), Rybelsus (semaglutide), Saxenda (liraglutide), Trulicity (dulaglitide), Victoza (liraglutide), Wegovy (semaglutide).

  • Do GLP-1R agonists help to lower A1c levels?

    Research shows that GLP-1R agonists help reduce A1c levels to about 1% in people with type 2 diabetes. These medications lower fasting blood sugar levels and reduce post-meal blood sugar spikes without increasing the risk of hypoglycemia.

  • Are there any oral GLP-1 agonists for people with type 2 diabetes?

    Yes, one example of an oral GLP-1 agonist is Rybelsus (semaglutide) that is FDA approved for the treatment of type 2 diabetes. Rybelsus may have some side effects on the patient some of which include abdominal pain, diarrhea, nausea, constipation, loss of appetite and vomiting.

  • Are there different types of GLP-1R agonists?

    Yes, two types of GLP-1R agonists exist, which are the short-acting formulas that are taken once or twice daily, and the long-acting formulas, which are taken once in a week. The type of GLP-1R agonist prescribed to the patient depends on certain factors like medical history, blood sugar control, cost (GLP-1R agonists can be expensive), and the patients preference.

  • What is the mod of administration of GLP-1R agonists?

    Most GLP-1R agonists are administered subcutaneously (injected). This means the agonists are given with a syringe and needle or with a pre-filled dosing pen, depending on the drug. These medications are injected into fatty tissue (either your abdomen or upper thigh) just below the surface of the skin.

  • Does the intake of GLP-1R agonists cause side effects?

    Generally, GLP-1R agonists are safe and may cause little to no side effects. Side effects like nausea, vomiting and diarrhea affect 10 to 40 percent of patients who take GLP-1R agonists. These side effects tend to be less severe the longer the patient takes the medications. In some rare cases some patients tend to have severe side effects such as ongoing abdominal or back pain, dizziness or fainting, extreme thirst, decreased urination, itching, pounding heart etc. such cases should be treated as an emergency if a patient starts to have them.
